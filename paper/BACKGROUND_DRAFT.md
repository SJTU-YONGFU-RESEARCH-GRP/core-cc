## Background

The semiconductor industry's continued advancement relies on Design-Technology Co-Optimization (DTCO), a methodology that integrates circuit design with manufacturing process development [1]-[3]. Fig. 1 depicts DTCO as a closed-loop feedback methodology for the precise optimization of process technology and circuit design [4]. The methodology involves technology computer-aided design (TCAD) simulations [5], [6], process integration [7]-[9], standard cell design [10]-[12], process design kit (PDK) development [13]-[15], circuit implementation, and performance-power-area (PPA) evaluation [16]. This entire flow is initiated by the Register-Transfer Level (RTL) circuit descriptions. As highlighted in Fig. 1, this RTL Design stage is the foundational input for the entire implementation and evaluation cycle. Consequently, the evaluation of the power-performance-area-cost (PPAC) is directly dependent on the quality, consistency, and diversity of the input RTL circuits used for benchmarking.

Addressing the need for a diverse set of benchmarks, our prior work introduced the CORE (Corpus Of RTL designs for EDA Research) project, a benchmark suite of 137 unique Verilog modules across 24 distinct categories [17]. While such broad-spectrum datasets are invaluable for general-purpose DTCO and PPAC analysis, they inherently lack the specialized depth required for domain-specific optimization. A particularly critical domain is fault-tolerant computing, where the design of Error-Correcting Codes (ECC) is fundamental to ensuring data integrity in memory, storage, and communication systems [18]. The intricate trade-offs between an ECC scheme's correction capability, latency, and hardware overhead demand a focused and highly-parameterized dataset to enable systematic co-optimization.

Error-correction code (ECC) IP constitutes a fundamental workload for DTCO across the communication, storage, and computing domains. The selection of a decoder architecture is ranging from block, convolutional, or graph-based codes to specific scheduling and parallelism strategies which creates distinct microarchitectural trade-offs. These design choices drive critical metrics such as logic depth, memory footprint, and control complexity, which propagate to timing, congestion, and power once synthesized and placed [19]. However, unlike the CPU domain (e.g., RISC-V benchmarks) or AI (e.g., MLPerf [20]), the ECC domain lacks a unified, open-source RTL benchmark suite. High-quality implementations are often proprietary [21] or developed in isolation for specific studies [24]-[26], often scattered across disparate repositories with inconsistent interfaces [27]-[29]. While comprehensive software simulation environments like AFF3CT [30], [31] provide robust algorithmic evaluation, they typically rely on C++ or Python and do not offer the synthesizable Verilog RTL required for hardware-level DTCO and PPAC analysis. Table I summarizes the contributions and limitations of these existing resources. This fragmentation forces researchers to rely on non-standardized baselines, complicating cross-family comparisons and limiting reproducibility.

**TABLE I: Overview of Existing ECC Resources: Contributions and Limitations**

| Resource Category | Representative Examples | Contribution | Limitation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Algorithmic Libraries** | `pycodes` [24], `reedsolomon` [25] | Provide accessible Python implementations for understanding and simulating basic ECC logic. | Purely software-based; lack hardware implementation details (timing, area, power) and synthesizable RTL. |
| **Isolated Hardware IP** | `verilog-LDPC-decoder` [27], `LDPC-Project` [28] | Offer optimized Verilog/VHDL implementations for specific complex codes (e.g., LDPC). | "Scattered" across repositories with inconsistent interfaces and verification methodologies; difficult to integrate into a unified flow. |
| **Software Frameworks** | `AFF3CT` [30], [31] | High-performance, standardized environment for algorithmic throughput and error rate simulation. | Relies on C++/Python; does not provide synthesizable RTL for physical design (PPA) analysis or hardware-software co-optimization. |

To address these limitations of cross-family comparisons and reproducibility, this paper presents CORE-CC, a dedicated, highly-parameterized ECC dataset built upon the CORE foundation. We transition from a general-purpose evaluation to the deep characterization of error correction and digital design through:

**Comprehensive and Specialized Library**: The framework provides a broad and deep foundation for ECC evaluation. It currently features over 25 unique Error-Correcting Codes, spanning fundamental schemes like Parity and Hamming, advanced algebraic codes such as BCH and Reed-Solomon, and modern capacity-approaching codes including LDPC, Turbo, and Polar. Unlike isolated implementations, this dataset unifies over **400 unique test configurations** across **4 million simulation trials** generated by the benchmarking framework. The open-source nature of the collection encourages community contributions and ensures its future growth.

**Parameterized and Scalable Designs**: All ECC modules are designed to be highly parameterized, allowing for systematic evaluation across different configurations. Key parameters such as `word_lengths` (e.g., 4, 8, 16, 32 bits) and `error_patterns` (e.g., single, double, burst, random) can be easily adjusted to generate a wide array of test scenarios. This supports scalability studies and enables a detailed analysis of performance trade-offs for various application requirements.

**Algorithm-to-Hardware Implementation, Verification, and Standardization**: A key differentiator of CORE-CC is its dual-domain implementation strategy, bridging the gap between software flexibility and hardware reality. The quality and correctness of the dataset are ensured by a rigorous multi-layered verification workflow. Each ECC module is first implemented and validated as a Python "golden reference" model. This model is then used to generate a corresponding Verilog implementation, which is subsequently verified through a co-simulation process using Verilator to ensure bit-perfect functional equivalence. Finally, all Verilog modules are synthesized with Yosys to confirm their hardware realizability and extract practical cost metrics. To ensure broad tool compatibility, modules are written using synthesizable constructs primarily from the Verilog-1995 (IEEE 1364-1995) [22] and SystemVerilog-2012 (IEEE 1800-2012) [23] standards.
